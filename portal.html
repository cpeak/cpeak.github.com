The Death of Portal: A Post-Mortem (v0.1)

History

Back in the early days of 8th Light we made a lot of our own software in house - numerous projects were constantly being worked on. Doppler was for sales, Abacus was for accounting, and many others. Death came for them all one by one. A number of us always wanted to showcase what 8th Light could do by having our own software to show off (or even offer it as a paid service for passive income), but as with the cobbler and the worn shoes we never got enough momentum and dedicated time to work on them and they were always on the back burner.

The Original Portal

Some time around 2014 I took up what we called a "second apprenticeship" on the development side, working closely with Craig D for a few months. We used Rails as a learning platform for me, where I would write smaller programs, do katas for the entire company, and learn the day to day skills a developer needs. One of those exercises turned into a family tree app called Lineage, just a simple database app keeping track of my family tree. After a few months of development I realized that 8th Light (at the time) was organized much the same, everyone who was hired went through the apprenticeship program, so we had a family tree of sorts. I wanted to capture not only that history, but also make something fun for us to use internally.

The Vision

Portal was to be a social hub for 8th Light. When we only had about 25 employees, it was easy to know who to ask about certain things - need Clojure help? Talk to Colin. I could get up and tap them on the shoulder. After a while as the company grew I realized nobody could keep that knowledge in their head, and new hires were just dropped into it all. So I wanted to track what people knew and what they wanted to know. Slack was to ephemeral, if you weren't in the right channels or on vacation you would miss important messages. How could we fix this? How about a place to list what we have learned and importantly what we can teach others in the company.

The Problem: Not Saying No

It was a one man show for a while, but it only lived on my laptop, so I needed help getting it live to the company. Ryan stepped in and we started working on it with developers as a learning exercise for them as well. Apprentices helped out when they could, developers pitched in when they were on the bench, but there was no consistency. Not long after launch the HR and Staffing teams realized that it might be a good tool to use in preparation for staffing crafters on projects. I never stopped to say no - I just started coming up with solutions. It didn't feel like a radical change at the time - we already had a yearbook type list of the company, lets just add the skills and filters so Kai could find what she needed. That morphed into 'how can this help in career growth and reviews'.

From then on we just kept shoe horning in features because the higher ups needed them. We wired it up to Slack, Metric, Rippling, and a number of other services. The app just kept getting bigger and bigger, clunkier and clunkier. It shifted from a tool for knowledge sharing to some weird HR/career abomination. I mean we did alright, it wasn't a turd by any means, we just built it on a very porous foundation.

Feature Parity - The Scope Killer

Another stake in the heart of Portal was scope creep due to everyone wanting feature parity with their workflow. We were trying to cram about 100 different spreadsheets from 20 different people into one app. Not once did I put my foot down and ask why, or what problems does this solve? I made good faith assumptions that the higher ups had already worked out these problems, I just needed to wire it up and get it on screen for them.

Whats next

I'm not sure how this rambling can help us, but I wanted this to be a brain dump (no AI!) and start shaking it to see if any nuggets of wisdom fall out. I still want a showcase app, I would love a SaaS product BY 8th Light for some passive income, and I really want a fun social app for 8th Light to play with and enjoy without getting taken over by the C-suite. More thoughts to come.
